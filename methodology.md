# Traction Signal Methodology — Anwen Labs

## What this analysis is, and is not

This report measures **statistical signals** associated with inorganic star growth on public GitHub repositories. It does **not** determine that any repository, maintainer, or organization purchased stars or engaged in fraud. Signals have benign explanations — a Hacker News front-page day produces a legitimate star burst; a viral tweet attracts many new accounts. Tiers express *how much the observed pattern deviates from organic baselines*, nothing more. Any decision-grade conclusion requires investigation beyond this method.

## Why these signals

The method is a lightweight adaptation of peer-reviewed research: He et al., *"Six Million (Suspected) Fake Stars in GitHub"* (arXiv:2412.13459, accepted at ICSE 2026), which identified ~6M suspected fake stars across 18,617 repositories using account-cluster and temporal signals — findings substantially validated when GitHub deleted over 90% of flagged repositories by January 2025. Context on why the problem matters: stars sell openly for $0.03–$0.85 each; a Redpoint analysis of 80 dev-tool companies put the median seed-stage star count at ~2,850 — under $200 of purchased credibility. The FTC's 2024 final rule prohibits the sale and purchase of fake social-media influence indicators, with civil penalties up to $53,088 per violation.

## Signals measured

**1. Temporal burst analysis.** Star events are grouped by day across the observed history. We report: burst ratio (max daily stars ÷ median nonzero daily stars), top-day share (fraction of observed stars landing on the single largest day), and spike days (days exceeding mean + 3σ). Coordinated campaigns concentrate stars in short windows; organic growth — even viral growth — decays over multiple days.

**2. Stargazer account-quality analysis.** Every stargazer account in the observed window is checked for: follower count, public repository count, and account age at the moment of starring (fields returned inline by the GraphQL API — no sampling step, so account statistics cover the full observed window). We report *weak-account share* (0 followers AND ≤1 public repo) and *young-weak share* (weak AND account younger than 90 days when it starred). Fake-star campaigns cluster in newly created, low-activity accounts.

**3. Adoption cross-check (manual).** Where the project ships a package, registry download counts (npm, PyPI, crates.io) are compared against star counts. He et al. found 70.46% of fake-starred packages had **zero** dependent packages — inflated visibility with no real usage. Reported as a ratio, not a verdict.

## Signal tiers

| Tier | Meaning | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 — baseline | Pattern consistent with organic growth | No condition met |
| Tier 2 — moderate | One elevated signal | Burst ratio ≥50 or top-day share ≥25%, **or** young-weak accounts ≥30% of sample |
| Tier 3 — elevated | Multiple concurrent signals | A burst condition **and** the account condition |
| Insufficient data | Not scored | <100 observed star events or <50 profiles checked |

Thresholds are deliberately conservative and published here precisely so they can be criticized. A Tier 3 designation means "this pattern warrants a closer look before treating the star count as a traction signal in a decision" — for example, in investment due diligence.

**Burst-window guard.** Burst conditions are only evaluated when the observed window spans at least 14 calendar days. A truncated capture of a single viral day is 100% top-day-share *by construction* — flagging it would manufacture false positives on legitimately viral projects (we verified this failure mode empirically before adding the guard). Windows narrower than 14 days are tiered on account quality alone. By default the analysis examines the **most recent** stars, where a campaign inflating a currently trending repository would appear; launch-era history can be examined with the `--direction oldest` option.

## Reproducibility

Every number in the report is generated by the open script (`orbit_check.py`) against the GitHub GraphQL API — no privileged data, no scraping. Anyone can re-run the analysis on any repository and check our output: the script authenticates via GitHub's device flow with a token that requests **zero account permissions** (or a personal access token, if you prefer).

**A note on data access.** On June 30, 2026 GitHub announced that the REST stargazers endpoint and stargazer UI views are being restricted to repository admins and collaborators, citing misuse of the data for spam. At the time of writing, the same star-event data (timestamps and account metadata) remains available to any authenticated caller through the GraphQL API, which is what this tool uses. We disclose this because reproducibility is the foundation of this methodology: if GraphQL access is later restricted as well, we will republish the method on the public GH Archive event stream (the original study's data source) and note the change here. Coverage limits are disclosed per repository (observed-window caps on very large projects). Corrections: maintainers who believe a signal is explained by a legitimate event (press coverage, HN front page, conference talk) can contact us with the date; verified explanations will be published alongside the tier.

## Limitations

Observed-window caps on very large repos (the default examines the most recent ~4,000 star events); no access to GitHub's internal signals (the original study's authors had richer data); deleted accounts are largely invisible in GraphQL responses (removed from the stargazer connection entirely), so purged campaigns are undercounted; the burst-window guard means very recent campaigns inside a <14-day window register only through account-quality signals. This method will produce false positives on legitimately viral projects and false negatives on slow-drip campaigns. That is why output is a signal tier, not an accusation.
